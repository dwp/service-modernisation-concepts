Change of bank details questions pages

This page explains research and design specific to the following set of pages:

  • Change of bank details pages:
    • Before you change your bank details
    • Your existing bank details
    • Enter your bank details
    • Check your bank details

What was the design intention?

  • The intention behind this concept was to explore a possible change of circumstances journey for updating bank details. It does not include the notifications a user would receive alongside the journey.
  • The journey was based on the Universal Credit flow with added two factor authentication.

Did we test it?

These pages were tested with 10 users in round 3 of research.

Detailed research summary

We decided against reanalysing the data from this research as it is very likely that this journey already exists elsewhere. If this has not happened yet, it should be based on designs already tested and used – for instance in Universal Credit or other benefits where people change their bank details and where they are in receipt of multiple benefits.

Considerations for further research and design work

  • Carry out further research to understand where best to offer further support within the interface, for example, to talk to an advisor.
  • Explore options for using information we already have as DWP about the customer's payment schedule to warn them about the money potentially entering the wrong bank account if they change their bank details just before a payment is due.
  • Where the user is asked to enter bank details, make it clearer that this is entering the new bank details.
  • Explore how to help the customer when entering bank details, for example whether hyphens can appear automatically when they enter the sort code.
  • Provide further guidance to help users, for example, explain what format a roll number would be in.
  • Check standard patterns for the check answers pages and ensure there is only one key action per page.
  • Explore how we can make numeric content work for users who struggle to understand numbers (e.g. users with dyscalculia), as well as work for users who struggle to read and may have dyslexia.
